The lady behind the black hole

For the first time in the history of science, we were able to take the picture of a mysterious black hole in a distant galaxy. The picture became so viral and we also heard the name ‘Katie Bouman’ and her ‘Algorithm’ in associated with the picture. Have you ever wondered what a computer scientist and her ‘algorithm’ has to do with taking the picture of a black hole! Let’s discuss…

Katie Louise Bouman is a 29 year old American computer scientist and works in the field of imaging. She was part of the Event Horizon Telescope Team who were trying to take the image of a black hole in the M87 galaxy which is 55 million light years away.

8 telescopes situated at different parts of the globe, which are linked precisely by atomic clocks were used to observe the black hole. Precise measurements were made by combining data from all the telescopes using super computers and calibrators. But what we get from these telescopes is simply measurements that are very confusing. To create a picture out of these measurements we need algorithms.

If there was telescopes all around the globe, the measurement would have been precise and the problem would be really trivial and simply need to apply inverse Fourier transform. But here we only have fewer samples with infinite number of possible images.

If there was telescopes all around the globe, the measurement would have been precise and the problem would be really trivial and simply need to apply inverse Fourier transform. But here we only have fewer samples with infinite number of possible images. Traditional algorithms used in Radio Interferometry is suitable only for long wavelengths. For shorter wave lengths from the event horizon of a black hole these algorithms are not enough. So the scientists came up with two classes of algorithms: Inverse Modelling (the traditional way) and the Forward modelling.

This is where Katie Bouman and her team came up with new algorithms which lead to the formation of the black hole image from the petabytes of data obtained from different telescopes from around the globe.
